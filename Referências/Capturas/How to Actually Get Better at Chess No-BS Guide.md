### Strategy #2

Always ask yourself what your opponent is threatening. One of the most common mistakes is only focusing on your own moves without thinking about what your opponent is trying to do. After every move your opponent makes, you should always ask yourself, what is the purpose of this move?
Maybe they're setting up a pawn break, or maybe there's a tactical threat coming. If you can figure out their plan, you'll be way better prepared to deal with it and avoid nasty surprises. So, let's say your opponent opens with one E4. You reply with one E5. Both of you develop your pieces and then they suddenly play queen to H5. If you don't stop and ask yourself, why did they bring the queen out so early? You might ignore the threat and you get hit with the scholars mate. And obviously, you don't want that.


### Strategy #3 - Don't trade your bishop for a knight without a good reason

. In chess, every piece is given a standard value.

Both a pair of knights and a pair of bishops are usually valued at six points, but it really depends on the position. In very closed positions, knights can be more effective because they can hop over pieces and create threats in tight spaces. That said, a lot of grand masters agree that compared to a pair of knights or a mixed pair, having both bishops is basically like having an extra point. That's because bishops control more squares from a distance and especially in the middle game. When the center starts to open up, they become a powerful weapon.

So avoid trading off your bishop early in the game. Do it only if you're taking out a really strong piece sitting on a key square. If the position is super close or if the trade gives you an immediate tactical advantage. Master clock timing.
